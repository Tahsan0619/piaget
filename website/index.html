Piaget's Theory

The Four Cognitive Stages

Each stage represents a qualitatively different way of thinking, with 10 specific indicators assessed.

0–2 yrs
Stage 1

Sensorimotor

Learning through senses and motor actions. Infants develop object permanence and begin to understand cause-effect relationships.

Key Indicators

Object Permanence Cause-Effect Goal-Directed Behavior Imitation Sensory Exploration Coordination of Actions Recognition Trial & Error Learning Anticipation Mental Representation

Sample Assessment

"When an object is hidden, does it still exist?"

2–7 yrs
Stage 2

Preoperational

Symbolic but illogical thinking. Children develop language and pretend play but struggle with logic, conservation, and taking others' perspectives.

Key Indicators

Symbolic Thinking Egocentrism Centration Conservation Animistic Thinking Irreversibility Intuitive Thought Pretend Play Perceptual Dominance Egocentric Speech

Sample Assessment

"Imagine a tree feels sad when its leaves fall. Is this true?"

7–11 yrs
Stage 3

Concrete Operational

Logical thinking about concrete objects. Children master conservation, classification, and transitive inference but struggle with abstract concepts.

Key Indicators

Conservation Reversibility Decentration Classification Seriation Transitive Inference Logical Operations Cause-Effect Reasoning Perspective-Taking Concrete Problem Solving

Sample Assessment

"If Tom is taller than Sara, and Sara is taller than Mike, who is the tallest?"

11+ yrs
Stage 4

Formal Operational

Abstract and hypothetical thinking. Adolescents reason about possibilities, use deductive logic, and consider multiple perspectives systematically.

Key Indicators

Abstract Thinking Hypothetical Reasoning Hypothetico-Deductive Systematic Problem Solving Metacognition Moral Reasoning Relativistic Thinking Propositional Logic Future Orientation Ideological Thinking

Sample Assessment

"Imagine a society where no rules existed. Which statement best represents sophisticated thinking about the consequences?"

🎯 Scoring Intelligence

80–100 — Achieved

Stage-appropriate reasoning. Demonstrates decentration, logical operations, or abstract thinking.

50–79 — Developing

Transitional thinking. Shows emerging understanding but lacks full logical structure.

0–49 — Not Yet Achieved

Pre-stage patterns. Centration, egocentrism, or concrete-only thinking detected.

📝 AI Prompt Architecture

The system prompt instructs the AI to act as an expert developmental psychologist specializing in Piaget's theory. It enforces:

🧠
Piaget-Aligned Precision

Each question directly assesses one specific cognitive criterion with options spanning pre-stage → developing → achieved thinking patterns.

🎓
Age-Appropriate Complexity

Language and scenarios automatically adapt: simple concrete for ages 2-7, logical relationships for 7-11, abstract/hypothetical for 11+.

📊
Intelligent Scoring Rubrics

Every MCQ option includes Piaget-specific cognitive markers in its scoring—not just right/wrong, but developmental reasoning analysis.

🔄
Uniqueness & Diversity

No two questions share scenarios or phrasing. Domains vary: physical, social, logical, symbolic, and moral reasoning.
